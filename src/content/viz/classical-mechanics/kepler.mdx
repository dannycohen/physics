## Farther out means a longer year

Drag the orbit wider and watch the period climb. A planet farther from the Sun
is doing two things at once, both of which slow it down. It has a longer path to
travel all the way around, and the Sun's pull out there is weaker, so it moves
more slowly along that longer path. Put those together and the year grows fast.

In Solar-System units the bookkeeping is clean. Measure distance in
astronomical units (AU), where 1 AU is the Earth-Sun distance, and time in
years. Then the law is simply `T² = a³`. Earth checks out at once: `1² = 1³`.
Mars at 1.52 AU gives a year of about 1.9; Jupiter at 5.2 AU takes almost 12;
Neptune way out at 30 AU takes about 165.

## Where the law comes from

The bare `T² = a³` hides a constant that happens to equal 1 in these units. The
fuller form is `T² = 4π²a³/(GM)`, where `G` is the gravitational constant and
`M` is the Sun's mass. Written that way the law is no coincidence: it falls
straight out of the same inverse-square gravity that holds any orbit together.
It also does real work. Measure a planet's `a` and `T`, and the full equation
hands you the mass of the body being orbited. That is how the Sun's mass, and
the masses of planets with moons, are weighed.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Do outer planets just move slower?</summary>

  Not just slower — they also have farther to go, and both effects push the same
  way, so the period grows faster than the distance does. Quadruple the orbit
  radius and the path length only doubles, but the planet also crawls along it
  more slowly, and the two combine to make the year eight times longer, not four.
  That steeper-than-linear growth is exactly what `T² = a³` encodes.
</details>
