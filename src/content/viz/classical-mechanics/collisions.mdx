## What is always true in a collision

Momentum is the mass of an object times its velocity, and the total momentum of
the two balls, added together, is the same before and after they hit. This holds
in every collision, no exceptions: a bouncy super-ball, two lumps of clay, cars
in a crash. Whatever momentum goes in comes back out. That single rule is why the
readout above always says "momentum conserved" no matter where you drag the
sliders.

## The one number that changes everything

Kinetic energy is a different story. How much of it survives the collision is set
by the coefficient of restitution `e`, the bounciness. It runs from 0 to 1.
At `e = 1` the collision is elastic and every joule of kinetic energy comes back
out. At `e = 0` the collision is perfectly inelastic: the balls stick together
and move off at a single speed, and the most energy possible is lost. Anything in
between keeps part of the energy. Slide the bounciness down and watch the "KE kept"
percentage fall while the momentum reading stays put.

When two equal masses collide elastically, they simply swap velocities. That is
the trick behind a Newton's cradle: the incoming ball stops dead and the one at
the far end flies off at the speed the first one had. Set both masses equal, leave
the bounciness at 1, and the before and after panels show exactly that trade.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Is energy always conserved in a collision?</summary>

  Momentum always is, but kinetic energy is only conserved when the collision is
  perfectly elastic, `e = 1`. Real collisions lose some kinetic energy to heat,
  sound, and permanent deformation, which is why a dropped ball never quite
  bounces back to the height it started from. The lower the bounciness, the more
  energy is lost, and at `e = 0` the loss is as large as it can be while still
  keeping momentum fixed. Total energy is still conserved overall; it just leaves
  the motion and turns into other forms.
</details>
