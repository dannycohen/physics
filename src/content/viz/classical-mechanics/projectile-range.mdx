## Why 45° goes the farthest

Range is a tug-of-war between two things a throw needs: horizontal speed to
cover ground, and time in the air to use that speed. Throw too flat and the
projectile is fast but lands almost immediately. Throw too steep and it hangs
forever but barely moves sideways. The best trade sits right in the middle, at
45°, where `sin(2θ)` reaches its largest value of 1. Slide the angle away from
45° in either direction and the range drops off.

## Complementary angles tie

Look at the `sin(2θ)` in the equation. Feed it an angle θ or its complement
90 − θ and you get the same number, because `sin(2θ)` and `sin(2(90 − θ))` are
equal. So a 20° throw and a 70° throw of the same speed land in exactly the same
spot. The steep one just spends much longer in the air getting there. Try the
"Low and fast" and "High lob" presets and watch the landing marker stay put.

## What this leaves out

The formula assumes level ground (launch and landing at the same height), no air
resistance, and a constant gravity `g`. Those idealizations are why the arc is a
clean parabola. Real throws land short of it: drag bleeds off speed, and a ball
launched from shoulder height lands below where it started, both of which nudge
the best angle a little under 45°.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Is steeper always farther?</summary>

  No. It helps only up to 45°. Past that, extra steepness trades away too much
  horizontal speed for airtime you cannot use, and the range falls again. That is
  why 30° and 60° reach the same distance, and why the flat 20° shot matches the
  steep 70° lob. Steeper buys you hang time, not distance.
</details>
