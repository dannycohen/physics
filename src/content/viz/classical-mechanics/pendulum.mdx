## Only the length matters

Drag the slider and watch the period readout. Just one thing on the right-hand
side of the equation, the length, is something you can change by rebuilding the
pendulum. Gravity `g` is fixed wherever you stand, and `2π` is just a number. So
for a given place on Earth, the swing time is set entirely by how long the rod
is.

Notice what is *not* in the formula. There is no mass. A lead bob and a cork bob
on rods of the same length keep exactly the same time. There is no starting
angle either, as long as you keep the swing small. Pull it back a little more
and it travels farther, but it also moves faster, and the two effects cancel.
That steadiness is the whole reason a pendulum can run a clock: the swing time
barely drifts as the swing slowly dies down.

The dependence on length is gentle. Because it sits under a square root, you
have to *quadruple* the length to *double* the period. A 1 m pendulum swings in
about 2 s; you need a 4 m pendulum to reach 4 s.

## The small-angle catch

The clean formula `T = 2π√(L/g)` assumes small swings. It comes from
approximating the restoring pull as proportional to the <abbr title="how far the bob is from its resting point at the bottom">displacement</abbr>, which only
holds near the bottom. For a genuinely large swing (tens of degrees), the period
grows slightly, and the neat independence from <abbr title="how far the bob is pulled back before you release it">amplitude</abbr> weakens. The animation
here holds a deliberately small swing so the approximation stays honest.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does a heavier bob swing slower?</summary>

  No. Swap in a bob twice as heavy and the period does not budge. Gravity pulls
  twice as hard on the heavier bob, but the heavier bob is also twice as hard to
  get moving, and those two effects cancel exactly, so mass drops out of the
  equation. It is the same reason Galileo saw a light and a heavy weight fall
  together: the acceleration due to gravity does not depend on mass.

  Amplitude is nearly as innocent. For small swings, pulling the bob back
  farther makes it cover more distance but also move faster, and the swing time
  stays put. Only once the angle gets large does the period creep upward.
</details>
