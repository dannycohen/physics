## Why two slits make bands

Light passing through each slit spreads out on the far side as if the slit were
a tiny source. Past the barrier the two spreading waves overlap, and where they
meet they add up. Where a crest from one slit lands on a crest from the other,
the light reinforces itself into a bright fringe. Where a crest meets a trough,
they cancel and you get darkness. The result is a row of evenly spaced bright
bands on the screen, not the two plain lines you might expect from two holes.

## Where the bright fringes land

A bright fringe appears wherever the path from one slit is longer than the path
from the other by a whole number of wavelengths. That condition is `d sin θ = m
λ`: the centre band is `m = 0` (straight ahead, equal paths), the next is `m =
1` (one wavelength of path difference), and so on. Read it as a competition
between `λ` and `d`. Make the wavelength longer, or push the slits closer
together, and each fringe swings out to a larger angle, so the pattern spreads.
Shorten the wavelength or widen the spacing and the fringes bunch toward the
centre.

Because `sin θ` can never exceed 1, only a finite number of orders fit: the
count is however many whole wavelengths `d` can hold, `m ≤ d / λ`. Slide to a
long wavelength with close slits and you may keep just the central band and one
order on each side; go the other way and a dozen orders crowd onto the screen.

This banded pattern is the classic proof that light travels as a wave, since
only waves interfere. Send particles through one at a time and the same fringes
still build up, which is how the experiment also demonstrates matter waves; see
the <a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/quantum/de-broglie/`}>de Broglie page</a>.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Do the slits just cast two bright lines?</summary>

  No. If light were simply a stream of particles going straight through, you
  would see two bright strips, one behind each slit. Instead the light spreads
  from each slit and the two spreading waves interfere, painting many evenly
  spaced fringes across the whole screen. Each bright band marks a spot where
  the path difference between the two slits is a whole number of wavelengths;
  the dark gaps between them are where the waves cancel.
</details>
