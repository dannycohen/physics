## Bunched ahead, spread behind

A moving source keeps emitting sound at a steady pitch, but it chases its own
wavefronts. Each new crest goes out from a point a little further along than the
last, so ahead of the source the crests bunch together and behind it they spread
apart. Crests arriving closer together mean a higher frequency; crests spread out
mean a lower one. Stand ahead of the source and you hear it sharp; stand behind
and you hear it flat.

The size of the shift depends on how fast the source moves compared with the
speed of sound in the medium — here, still air at 343 m/s. Push the source speed
toward that value and the wavefronts ahead pile up almost on top of each other,
so the pitch you hear climbs steeply. This picture assumes the source moves
straight along the line to you and the air is still.

## Sound has a medium; light does not

The everyday siren Doppler and the <a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/relativity/relativistic-doppler/`}>relativistic Doppler for light</a>
look alike: approaching raises the pitch or reddens to blue, receding does the
reverse. But they are not the same formula. Sound travels through air, a medium,
so it matters whether the source or the listener is the one moving relative to
that air, and the two cases give slightly different shifts. Light in a vacuum has
no medium: only the relative speed matters, the two observers agree symmetrically,
and the relativistic version also folds in time dilation, which the sound formula
has no reason to include.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does the siren itself change pitch as it passes?</summary>

  No. The siren emits the same steady pitch the whole time, and the driver sitting
  with it hears that true pitch unchanged. Only a listener the source is moving
  relative to hears a shift, and it flips from high to low the moment the source
  passes: approaching, the crests crowd together and the pitch is high; the instant
  it goes by and starts receding, the crests stretch out and the pitch drops. What
  you hear is a property of the geometry between you and the source, not a change in
  the source itself.
</details>
