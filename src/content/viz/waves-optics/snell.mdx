## Why does light bend?

Light travels slower through some materials than others, and the refractive
index is just a measure of how much a material slows it down. Air is close to 1,
water is about 1.33, ordinary glass about 1.5. When a ray crosses from one
material into another, the change in speed makes it change direction at the
boundary. That kerb is what you see when a straw in a glass of water looks
snapped in two.

Which way it bends follows one rule. Entering a slower, denser medium (a higher
index), the ray bends toward the normal, the dashed line drawn straight through
the surface. Entering a faster, lighter medium (a lower index), it bends away
from the normal. The steeper the incoming angle, the more there is to bend.

## When light can't get out

Going the other way, from a dense medium out to a lighter one, the ray bends
away from the normal and keeps opening up as you raise the incident angle. There
is a limit. At the critical angle the refracted ray would lie flat along the
surface, and beyond it there is no angle left for light to escape into: the
equation asks for `sin θ₂` greater than 1, which has no real answer. All of the
light reflects back into the dense medium instead. That is total internal
reflection, and it happens exactly when

`sin θ_c = n₂ / n₁`  (only possible when n₁ is greater than n₂).

It is not a defect. It is how a fibre-optic cable pipes light around bends for
kilometres without leaking, and why a cut diamond, with its very high index,
traps and throws back so much light that it sparkles.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does light always pass through into the next material?</summary>

  No. Going from a denser medium into a lighter one, once the incident angle
  passes the critical angle, none of the light gets through: it is 100% reflected
  back. That is total internal reflection. Try the "Glass to air (TIR)" preset
  and watch the transmitted ray vanish, replaced by a reflected ray and a label.
</details>
