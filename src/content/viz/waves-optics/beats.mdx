## Two waves add up, point by point

When two waves pass through the same place at the same time, they add: at every
instant the combined displacement is just the sum of the two. That is
superposition. Slide the two frequencies together and watch the bottom trace —
it is the top two traces added together, moment by moment.

When the frequencies are close, the two waves drift slowly in and out of step.
For a while their crests line up and reinforce, so the sum grows tall. A moment
later one has slipped half a cycle behind the other, crest meets trough, and they
cancel to almost nothing. That drifting produces the swelling and fading you can
see traced by the faint dashed outline: the **beat**.

## The beat rate is the difference; the pitch is the average

The sum swells and fades once for every cycle that one wave gains on the other,
so the loud-soft pulses come at exactly the difference of the two frequencies:

`f_beat = |f₁ − f₂|`

Two tones four hertz apart beat four times a second. That is why musicians tune
by ear: sound a reference note against the string, and adjust until the beating
slows to a crawl and then stops. No beats means the two frequencies are equal.

Keep the beat rate separate from the pitch. The pitch you hear is the **average**
of the two frequencies, `(f₁ + f₂)/2`, and it barely moves while the beat rate
changes. With 440 and 444 you hear a 442 Hz tone throbbing four times a second,
not a 4 Hz sound.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Do you hear a third new frequency at 4 Hz?</summary>

  No. Nothing in the sound actually vibrates at 4 Hz, and 4 Hz is far too low to
  hear as a pitch anyway. What changes at 4 Hz is the *loudness*: the single
  average tone gets louder and softer four times a second. The beat is a rhythm in
  the volume of one tone, not a separate tone added alongside the other two.
</details>
