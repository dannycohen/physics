## Shorter, but only in one direction

Length contraction squeezes a moving object along the direction it travels, and
only that direction. A ship flying past you nose-first looks shorter from front
to back, but just as tall and just as wide as ever. Turn the speed up and the
squeeze grows; at everyday speeds it is far too small to notice.

The effect is real, not a trick of the light or a delay in when signals reach
you. If you could lay a tape measure along the moving ship as it passed, you
would genuinely record a shorter number. And it is mutual: someone riding on the
ship measures *your* ruler as the contracted one, because each of you sees the
other rushing past.

## Same γ, working the other way

The contraction factor is `1/γ`, built from the very same Lorentz factor that
stretches time. Time dilation multiplies by γ; length contraction divides by it.
One number, two mirror-image effects. See γ on its own on the{' '}
<a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/foundations/lorentz-factor/`}>Lorentz factor page</a>.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does the object get crushed or squished?</summary>

  No. Nothing is compressed and no force acts on the ship — its crew feels
  perfectly normal and measures the ship at its full 100 m. Length itself is what
  depends on who is measuring. In the frame where the ship moves, the distance
  between its nose and tail is simply smaller. Slow back down and the measured
  length returns to normal, with nothing bent or broken.
</details>
