## Why does moving change time?

Imagine the simplest clock you can build: two mirrors facing each other, with a
flash of light bouncing between them. Every bounce is one tick. Nothing about
this clock is special: any clock riding along with it - a wristwatch, your
heartbeat - stays in step with it.

Now put that clock on a fast-moving ship and watch it from the ground. Between
one bounce and the next, the mirrors slide sideways, so from where you stand
the flash travels a diagonal - a longer path than the straight up-and-down hop
it makes on the ship.

Here is the strange part, confirmed by every experiment ever done: light always
moves at the same speed, no matter who measures it. A longer path at the same
speed takes more time. So you see the ship's clock tick slower than your own.
The clock is not broken and nothing on the ship feels different. From your
point of view, each tick simply takes longer.

At everyday speeds the diagonal is barely longer than the vertical hop, which
is why you have never noticed this. The effect only becomes visible when the
sideways motion is a decent fraction of the speed of light - roughly half of c
before it amounts to much.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Doesn't the moving clock just <em>look</em> slow?</summary>

  No - this is not an optical illusion or a trick of signal delay. The moving
  clock really does tick slower as measured in your frame. Stranger still, an
  observer riding with that clock measures <em>your</em> clock running slow,
  and both of you are right, because you disagree about which distant events
  happen at the same time.

  The muon preset above is physical proof. Muons created by cosmic rays high in
  the atmosphere decay so fast that, without time dilation, almost none would
  survive the trip down. They reach the ground in huge numbers anyway - their
  internal clock genuinely runs slow in our frame.
</details>
