## Deeper in the well, slower the clock

Gravity slows time. A clock deep in a gravity well ticks slower than one far
away, and the closer it sits to the mass, the slower it runs. This is not about
motion: the clock can be sitting perfectly still. What matters is how deep in
the gravity it rests.

The Schwarzschild radius r_s is the point of no return around a black hole. As
a clock approaches it, the rate drops toward zero, so to a distant observer the
clock appears to freeze. You never see it cross; it just fades and dims forever.

Near Earth the effect is tiny but real. A clock on the ground runs a hair slower
than one in orbit because it sits deeper in Earth's gravity. GPS satellites
correct for it every day. This is the opposite-sign partner to the speed effect
on the <a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/relativity/time-dilation/`}>special-relativity
time-dilation page</a>: motion slows a satellite's clock, while its weaker gravity
speeds it up, and engineers account for both.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Would you feel your own clock slow down?</summary>

  No. Your own clock always ticks normally to you. A second feels like a second,
  your heartbeat feels ordinary, and every clock riding along with you agrees.
  The slowing is what a <em>distant</em> observer measures when they compare your
  clock with theirs. Time dilation is always a comparison between frames, never
  something you feel happening to yourself.
</details>
