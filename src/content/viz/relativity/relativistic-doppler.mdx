## Approaching makes it bluer, receding makes it redder

Slide the speed and watch the bottom wave. It is the same light as the top wave,
seen from a different place. When the source moves toward you the crests pile up,
the wavelength shrinks, the frequency climbs, and the colour slides toward blue.
When the source moves away the crests spread out, the wavelength grows, the
frequency drops, and the colour slides toward red. The number in the readout is
the observed frequency written as a multiple of the source's: `2×` means twice as
high, `0.5×` means half.

This is the **longitudinal** version, the shift along the line of sight — the
source coming straight at you or going straight away. It is the shift that lets
astronomers read how fast a galaxy is receding straight from the reddening of its
light.

## What about moving sideways?

If the source flies past you at the closest point of its path, moving neither
toward nor away in that instant, there is still a shift — but a smaller one, and
always toward the red. That transverse case is pure time dilation: the source's
clock runs slow, so its light arrives at a lower frequency, `f_obs = f_src / γ`.
The formula on this page is the head-on case, where that time-dilation slowdown
is combined with the crest-piling of ordinary motion.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Is this just the ambulance-siren Doppler?</summary>

  It is the same idea — approaching raises the pitch, receding lowers it — but not
  the same formula. The siren travels through air, a medium, so the shift depends
  on whether it is the source or the listener that moves relative to that air.
  Light in a vacuum has no medium, so only the *relative* speed matters and the
  two observers agree symmetrically. The relativistic formula also folds in time
  dilation, which the everyday sound version has no reason to include.
</details>
