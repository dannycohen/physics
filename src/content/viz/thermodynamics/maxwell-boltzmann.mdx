## Why don't all the molecules move at the same speed?

Drop a billion molecules in a box and let them bounce off each other. Every
collision trades a bit of speed from one molecule to another: one comes away
faster, the other slower. After countless collisions the gas settles into a
steady spread of speeds, not a single value. That spread is the curve above,
and it depends only on the temperature and the mass of the molecules.

## The shape is a tug of war

The curve is a product of two competing factors. Slide the temperature and
watch which one wins at each end.

At low speeds the `v²` factor dominates: there are very few ways to move slowly,
so the count climbs from zero. At high speeds the exponential factor takes over:
fast molecules carry a lot of energy, and high energy is rare, so the count
falls away in a long thin tail. The peak sits where the two effects balance,
which is why the most probable speed is neither zero nor the fastest.

Because the tail leans to the right, the average speed and the root-mean-square
speed both sit a bit above the peak. That order never changes: most probable,
then average, then rms. (The root-mean-square speed is found by squaring every
molecule's speed, averaging those, then taking the square root; because squaring
weights the fast molecules more, it sits a little above the plain average.)

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Is the peak the average speed?</summary>

  No. The peak marks the *most probable* speed, the one the largest group of
  molecules happens to have. The average is higher, because the long fast tail
  drags it upward, and the root-mean-square speed is higher still. All three
  scale with the square root of temperature, so heating the gas moves every one
  of them to the right together.
</details>
