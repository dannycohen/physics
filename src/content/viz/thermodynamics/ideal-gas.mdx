## Where does pressure come from?

A gas is a swarm of molecules flying in every direction. Pressure is nothing more
than those molecules drumming on the walls: each tiny bounce is a push, and the
push per unit area, summed over billions of hits a second, is the pressure you
feel. Change how hard or how often they hit and the pressure changes with it.

Heating the gas speeds the molecules up, so they strike the walls harder and more
often. That is the same spread of speeds you can watch in the
[Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution](maxwell-boltzmann). Squeezing the gas into a
smaller volume packs the molecules closer to the walls, so the hits come thicker
and faster. Adding more gas simply puts more molecules in the box. Every one of
those moves raises the pressure, and `PV = nRT` is the bookkeeping that ties the
four quantities together: fix any three and the fourth is set.

## Reading the equation

Rearranged as `P = nRT / V`, the law reads directly off the sliders. Pressure
grows in step with the amount of gas and with temperature, and it falls as the
volume opens up. Double the temperature and the pressure doubles; halve the
volume and the pressure doubles again. `R` never moves; it is just the constant
that makes the units agree.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Is "ideal" real?</summary>

  Real gases follow this law closely under everyday conditions, room temperature
  and ordinary pressure, which is why it is so useful. It leans on two
  simplifications: that molecules take up no space of their own, and that they
  ignore each other between collisions. Those assumptions hold until you push a
  gas toward condensing, cool it near its boiling point, or crush it to very high
  pressure. There the molecules crowd and start to attract one another, and the
  real pressure drifts away from `nRT / V`.
</details>
