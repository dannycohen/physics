## Why hot things glow, and change colour as they heat up

Every object gives off light just because it is warm. A cool object glows only
in the far infrared, which you feel as heat but cannot see. Heat it up and two
things happen at once: the glow gets brighter at every wavelength, and the
wavelength it glows most strongly at gets shorter. Short means bluer. That is
why an iron bar goes from dull red to orange to yellow-white as it heats.

## Wien's law: bluer when hotter

The peak of the curve sits at a wavelength that is inversely proportional to
temperature. Double the temperature and the peak wavelength halves. Slide the
temperature and watch the dashed peak marker march to the left as the gas heats.
The Sun, at about 5778 K, peaks near 500 nm, right in the middle of the range
your eyes can see. A cooler filament peaks off to the right, in the infrared;
a hotter star peaks off to the left, toward the blue.

## No ultraviolet catastrophe

Classical physics predicted that a hot object should pour out ever more energy
at shorter and shorter wavelengths, without limit. It does not. The exponential
denominator, `e^{hc/λk_BT}`, blows up as the wavelength shrinks, so the radiance
falls back to zero on the short-wavelength side instead of running away. The
`λ⁵` in the front pushes it down further. Planck had to assume light comes in
discrete quanta to get this right, and that assumption started quantum physics.

## Brightness climbs steeply

The total brightness is the area under the whole curve, and it rises with the
fourth power of temperature. So a body twice as hot is not twice as bright: it
is about sixteen times brighter. The real curves span so many orders of
magnitude that a cool one would vanish next to a hot one, so the plot rescales
each temperature to its own peak — you are seeing the shape and the colour
shift, while this fourth-power growth in true brightness happens off the chart.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does a hotter object just get brighter?</summary>

  No. It gets brighter *and* bluer. The whole spectrum shifts, not just its
  height. If temperature only scaled the brightness, a heated bar would stay red
  and simply glow more intensely. Instead its peak wavelength slides toward the
  blue, so the colour you see changes from red to orange to white as it climbs.
  Wien's law tracks that shift; the fourth-power growth tracks the brightness.
</details>
