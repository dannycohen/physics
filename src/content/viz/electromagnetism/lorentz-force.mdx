## A force at right angles

The magnetic force on a moving charge is `q v × B`. That cross product is the
whole trick: it always comes out perpendicular to the velocity. Because the
force is sideways to the motion, it never pulls in the direction the charge is
already going, so it cannot speed the charge up or slow it down. All it can do
is turn it. A steady turn at constant speed is a circle, and the charge settles
into a loop of radius `r = m v / (q B)`.

## What changes the circle

Two knobs set how tight that loop is. A faster charge carries more momentum, so
it resists the turn and sweeps a wider circle. A stronger field bends the path
harder, pulling the charge into a smaller one. Flip the sign of the charge, or
reverse the field, and the force points the other way, so the charge circles in
the opposite direction. This is exactly how a cyclotron holds particles on a
looping path and how a mass spectrometer sorts <abbr title="an atom or molecule that carries an electric charge">ions</abbr> by how sharply they curve.

## No work, no energy change

Because the force stays perpendicular to the motion, it does no work. Work is
force along the direction of travel, and here there is none. So the charge's
kinetic energy, and therefore its speed, never changes in a purely magnetic
field. Only an electric field can add or remove energy. A magnetic field is a
steering wheel, not an accelerator pedal.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does a magnetic field speed the charge up?</summary>

  No. The magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it only
  changes the direction of motion, never the speed. The charge goes around and
  around at a constant pace; its kinetic energy stays fixed. If something is
  genuinely speeding a charged particle up, there is an electric field doing
  that work, not the magnetic field.
</details>
