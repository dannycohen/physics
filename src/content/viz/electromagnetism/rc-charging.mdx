## Charging through a resistor

Connect a battery to a capacitor through a resistor and the capacitor does not fill
up all at once. The resistor limits the current, so charge dribbles onto the
capacitor's plates. As the voltage builds, the gap between the capacitor and the
supply shrinks, the current falls, and charging slows down. The result is a curve
that shoots up at the start and then flattens as it eases toward the full voltage.

## The rate is set by the time constant

One number governs the whole curve: the time constant τ = <abbr title="the resistor–capacitor pair; their product R×C is the time constant τ">RC</abbr>. Multiply the
resistance in ohms by the capacitance in farads and you get τ in seconds. It is the
natural clock of the circuit. After one τ the capacitor has reached 63% of full
voltage. After two τ it is at about 86%, after three about 95%, and by five τ it is
at roughly 99% — close enough to call it full. Bigger R or bigger C means a bigger
τ and slower charging; smaller values fill the capacitor faster.

Each time constant closes about 63% of whatever gap is still left, so the same
shape repeats at every scale. That is why the table reads the same whether τ is a
millisecond or a second.

## The mirror image of exponential decay

This rising curve is the flip side of [exponential decay](/physics/viz/foundations/exponential-decay/).
A capacitor *discharging* through a resistor loses its voltage on a falling
exponential with the very same time constant τ = RC. Charging is that curve turned
upside down: instead of falling from full toward zero, it rises from zero toward
full. Same time-constant idea, same 63%-per-τ rhythm, just pointing the other way.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does it ever reach 100%?</summary>

  No. The capacitor approaches the full supply voltage forever, getting arbitrarily
  close but never landing exactly on it. Each time constant covers 63% of the
  remaining gap, so the gap shrinks and shrinks but never closes. In practice, after
  about five τ the last fraction of a percent is too small to notice and we call it
  fully charged — but mathematically it is like the decay curve that never quite
  hits zero, just seen from the other side.
</details>
