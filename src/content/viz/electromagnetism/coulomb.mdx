## Why do charges push and pull?

Electric charge comes in two kinds, which we label positive and negative. Two
charges of the same kind push each other away; two of opposite kinds pull
together. The grid of arrows above is the electric field: it shows which way a
tiny positive test charge would be shoved at each point in space.

## The inverse-square law

Read the equation as a fraction. The charges sit on top: double either one and
the force doubles, so the strength scales with how much charge is in play. The
distance sits on the bottom, and it is squared. That square is the whole story
of how the force fades. Move the charges twice as far apart and the force does
not drop to a half, it drops to a quarter. Slide them half as far apart and the
force does not double, it quadruples. The constant `k` out front is enormous,
which is why a few millionths of a coulomb already produce a force you could
feel in your hand.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does the bigger charge push harder on the smaller one?</summary>

  No. The two charges always feel forces of exactly the same size, in opposite
  directions, no matter how lopsided they are. The equation is symmetric in
  `q₁` and `q₂`: swap them and `F` is unchanged. Try the lopsided preset above,
  a strong positive next to a weak negative, and watch both force arrows stay
  the same length. That is Newton's third law showing up in electricity.
</details>
