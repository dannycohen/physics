## A constant fraction, not a constant amount

The defining feature of exponential decay is that the same *fraction* vanishes in
every equal stretch of time, no matter how much is there to begin with. Pick the
half-life: after that long, exactly half is gone. Wait the same time again and half
of what remains is gone, leaving a quarter. Again, an eighth. The amount lost each
step shrinks because there is less to lose, but the fraction never changes.

That is why the curve falls fast at first and then trails off, hugging the axis. It
approaches zero but never arrives: half of something is still something.

## Half-life versus mean lifetime

Two numbers describe the same curve. The half-life t½ is the time for half of what
is present to disappear. The mean lifetime τ is the average time a single item
survives, and it is the time constant in the tidy form N = N₀·e^(−t/τ). They are not
the same: τ = t½ / ln2 ≈ 1.44·t½, so the average life is a little longer than the
half-life. After one mean lifetime a fraction 1/e (about 37%) is left, not half.

This one shape governs far more than radioactivity. A capacitor discharging through
a resistor (the RC circuit) loses voltage the same way, with τ = RC. A drug clears
from the bloodstream on the same curve, which is why doctors talk about its half-life.
Same maths, different N₀ and τ.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>After two half-lives, is it all gone?</summary>

  No. After one half-life, 50% is left; after two, 25%; after three, 12.5%. Each
  half-life removes half of *what is left*, not half of the original amount, so the
  total never reaches zero. It keeps halving forever, getting arbitrarily close to
  zero while always leaving a little behind.
</details>
