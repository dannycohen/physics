## One factor behind it all

The Lorentz factor γ is the single number that turns Newton's physics into
Einstein's. It is built from just one ingredient: how fast you are going as a
fraction of the speed of light. Square that fraction, subtract it from 1, take
the square root, and flip it over. That is the whole recipe.

The shape of the curve is the point. For everyday speeds, even a fast jet, v/c
is so tiny that γ sits at 1.000 and relativity is invisible. Push past about
half the speed of light and γ starts to climb. In the last few percent before c
it shoots up without limit, because the square root in the denominator is racing
toward zero.

## Where γ shows up

The same γ multiplies several different quantities, which is why one page can
stand behind many:

- **Time dilation** — a moving clock's ticks stretch by γ. See it move on the{' '}
  <a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/relativity/time-dilation/`}>time dilation page</a>.
- **Length contraction** — a moving object shortens by a factor of 1/γ. See it on the{' '}
  <a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/relativity/length-contraction/`}>length contraction page</a>.
- **Energy** — a moving mass carries energy γmc², which is why reaching c would
  take infinite energy. See it on the{' '}
  <a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/relativity/mass-energy/`}>mass–energy page</a>.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Can γ ever be less than 1?</summary>

  No. γ is always at least 1, and equals 1 only when the object is at rest.
  Motion can stretch time and shrink length, but never the reverse: from your
  point of view a moving clock never ticks *faster* and a moving ruler never
  grows *longer*. The square root is always between 0 and 1, so one divided by
  it is always 1 or more.
</details>
