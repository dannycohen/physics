## Walking through walls

Classically, a ball rolled at a hill it cannot crest just rolls back down. A
particle without enough energy to get over a potential barrier does the same: it
bounces off. Quantum mechanics breaks that rule. The particle is described by a
<abbr title="the wave that describes where a quantum particle might be found">wavefunction</abbr>, and when it hits the barrier the wave does not stop dead. It leaks
into the wall, dying away as it goes, and a small piece survives to the far
side. Wherever the wave has any amplitude, the particle has some chance of being
found. So there is a nonzero probability the particle simply appears on the other
side, having tunnelled through a wall it could never climb.

The transmission probability `T` is the fraction that gets through. Inside the
barrier the wave decays at a rate `κ = √(2m(V₀−E))/ħ`, and the thicker the wall
the more that decay compounds. That is why `T` falls off roughly exponentially
with the barrier width `a`, and why it also drops as the energy `E` falls further
short of the top `V₀`. Drag the sliders: widen the barrier a touch and watch the
transmitted wave collapse to almost nothing. (The sinh in the formula is the
hyperbolic sine — you don't need its exact form; the point is that the
transmission falls off extremely fast as the barrier gets thicker.)

The formula on this page is the exact result for an idealised rectangular
barrier with the particle energy below the top (`E < V₀`), the regime where
tunnelling happens. It is not an approximation: it comes straight from matching
the wavefunction across both walls.

## Where this shows up

Tunnelling is not a curiosity. Alpha decay is an <abbr title="a clump of two protons and two neutrons — a helium nucleus">alpha particle</abbr> tunnelling out
through the barrier that binds it inside a nucleus, and the steep width
dependence is why decay half-lives span such an enormous range. A scanning
tunnelling microscope holds a sharp tip a fraction of a nanometre above a
surface and measures the tiny tunnelling current across the gap; because that
current changes so violently with distance, the instrument can map individual
atoms.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Does the particle "borrow" energy to jump over the wall?</summary>

  No. The particle never has more energy than `E`, and it never gets up to the
  top of the barrier, let alone over it. Nothing is borrowed and no debt is
  repaid. What happens is purely about the wave: the wavefunction has a small but
  real amplitude on the far side of the barrier, so when you look for the
  particle it sometimes turns up there. It did not climb the wall or leap it — it
  was simply, with some probability, already on the other side.
</details>
