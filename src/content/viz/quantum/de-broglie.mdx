## Matter is made of waves too

In 1924 Louis de Broglie proposed that the wave–particle duality already known
for light runs both ways: anything with momentum also has a wavelength. Slide the
electron's speed and watch the wave on the canvas. As the electron speeds up its
wavelength shrinks, so more crests crowd into the same width and the wave grows
tiny next to the fixed bar marking an atom's width.

The rule is `λ = h / p`, wavelength equals the Planck constant divided by
momentum. Momentum is `m·v`, so the wavelength gets shorter whenever the particle
is heavier or faster. Everything hinges on how small `h` is: about
`6.6 × 10⁻³⁴` joule-seconds. Divide that by any everyday momentum and you get a
number far too small to ever notice.

## Why the electron diffracts but you don't

A person walking at a stroll carries a de Broglie wavelength of roughly
`10⁻³⁵` metres — smaller than the size of a proton by twenty-odd orders of
magnitude, utterly beyond any possible measurement. That is why a walking person
never shows any hint of being a wave.

An electron is different. It is roughly `10³¹` times lighter than a person, so its
wavelength lands right around the size of an atom, near `10⁻¹⁰` metres. That is
big enough to interfere and <abbr title="bend and spread when passing through a regular pattern, producing wave interference">diffract</abbr> when it passes through the regularly spaced
atoms of a crystal, exactly as X-rays do. It is also what makes electron
microscopes work: a shorter wavelength resolves finer detail, and a fast
electron's wavelength is far shorter than any wavelength of visible light, so an
electron microscope sees things a light microscope never could.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>If everything is a wave, why don't I see myself ripple?</summary>

  Your wavelength is real, it is just hopelessly small — around `10⁻³⁵` metres,
  set by your large mass and ordinary walking speed. To see wave behaviour you
  need the wavelength to be comparable to the things it interacts with, and
  nothing in the universe is fine enough to reveal a ripple that small. Only
  light, low-mass things like electrons have wavelengths big enough to matter,
  which is why quantum weirdness shows up for them and not for you.
</details>
