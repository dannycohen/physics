## Two spreads on one seesaw

Δx and Δp are not the position and momentum themselves. They are the *standard
deviations* — the statistical spreads — of where a particle would be found and
how fast it would be measured to move if you prepared the same quantum state
over and over and measured it many times. A small Δx means the particle is
bunched into a narrow region; a small Δp means its momentum is bunched into a
narrow range.

Heisenberg's result is that the product of the two spreads has a floor:

Δx · Δp ≥ ħ/2

The curves above are a minimum-uncertainty packet, the tightest a quantum state
is allowed to be, so it rides right on that floor: Δx · Δp = ħ/2 exactly. Drag
the slider and watch the seesaw. Narrow the position curve into a tall spike and
the momentum curve fans out wide and low. Let the position spread out and the
momentum curve pulls in sharp. One can never get narrow without the other
getting wide.

## This is not about disturbing the particle

Here is the point almost everyone gets wrong. The uncertainty principle is **not**
a statement about clumsy measuring instruments knocking the particle around, and
it is **not** a claim that the act of measuring position ruins your knowledge of
momentum. Even in principle, with perfect instruments that touch nothing, a
quantum state simply does not possess a sharp position and a sharp momentum at
the same time.

It is a fact about what the particle *is*, not about what we are able to find
out. A state with a narrow position spread is built, mathematically, from a wide
spread of momenta; there is no state hiding underneath with both pinned down
that our tools are too crude to reach. Narrowing one spread necessarily widens
the other, because they are two faces of the same wave.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Is this just because measuring disturbs the particle?</summary>

  No. The disturbance story is a common but wrong picture. It suggests there is a
  real, definite position *and* a real, definite momentum sitting there, and that
  poking at one merely spoils our reading of the other. Quantum mechanics says
  something stronger: the particle does not have both a sharp position and a
  sharp momentum to begin with. The spread is a property of the state itself, not
  a smudge left by a heavy-handed instrument. Perfect measurement tools would not
  rescue you — there is simply nothing sharper underneath to measure.
</details>
