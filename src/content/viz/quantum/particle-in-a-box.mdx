## Why only certain energies?

Trap a particle in a box and it stops behaving like a marble that can roll at any
speed. It can only hold a short list of energies, drawn above as the rungs of a
ladder. Pick a level with the slider and the box highlights it. The lowest rung is
the ground state; the ones above it are the only other energies the particle is
ever allowed to have.

The rungs are not evenly spaced. Level `n` sits at `n²` times the ground energy, so
they go 1, 4, 9, 16, 25. That is why the gaps widen as you climb: the jump from
level 1 to 2 is smaller than the jump from 4 to 5.

## Standing waves in a box

The energies come from fitting waves into the box. The particle is also a
<a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/quantum/de-broglie/`}>matter wave</a>, and
only whole numbers of half-waves fit neatly between the two walls: one hump for
`n=1`, two for `n=2`, and so on. Each
allowed wave has its own wavelength, and a shorter wavelength means more energy,
which is exactly why the levels climb.

Squeeze the box smaller and every wave has to bend more sharply to fit, so every
level jumps up. That is the reason confinement costs energy: pinning a quantum
particle into a smaller space forces it to carry more.

## It can never sit still

Notice the bottom rung is above the `E = 0` line. A trapped quantum particle can
never have zero energy — it always keeps a minimum, the ground-state energy. This
is tied to
<a href={`${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}viz/quantum/heisenberg/`}>Heisenberg's uncertainty
principle</a>: pin the
particle inside a box and you have limited how much you can also pin down its
motion, so it can never be perfectly at rest.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Can it have any energy in between the rungs?</summary>

  No. The particle can only have the energies on the ladder — the `n²` rungs and
  nothing between them. There is no allowed state at, say, halfway between level 1
  and level 2. That is what quantization means, and it is the heart of quantum
  mechanics: energy in a bound system comes in a discrete set, not a continuous
  range.
</details>
