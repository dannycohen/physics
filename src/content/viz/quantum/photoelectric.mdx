## Light arrives in lumps

Classical physics said light was a wave, and a wave should be able to shake an
electron loose if you just poured in enough energy: turn up the brightness and
wait. That is not what happens. Shine dim blue light on a metal and electrons
pop out at once. Shine intense red light on the same metal and nothing comes
out, no matter how long you wait or how bright the beam.

Einstein's insight was that light comes in packets called photons, and one
photon carries an energy fixed only by its frequency: `E = hf`. A single photon
ejects a single electron, all at once, or not at all. To escape, that photon
must carry at least the metal's work function `φ` — the energy cost of pulling
an electron free. Anything left over becomes the electron's kinetic energy:
`K_max = hf − φ`.

## Colour sets the energy, brightness sets the count

Move the wavelength slider toward blue and the photon energy climbs; the ejected
electrons carry more kinetic energy. Move it toward red and the energy per photon
falls. Cross the threshold and emission stops dead.

Brightness is a separate knob entirely. A brighter beam is simply more photons
per second, so it ejects more electrons per second, but each electron still
leaves with exactly the same maximum energy, because each is freed by one photon
of the same frequency. More photons, not more energetic ones. This split, energy
from colour and count from brightness, is the fingerprint of the photon.

<details class="misconception">
  <summary>Won't a bright enough dim-colour light eventually eject electrons?</summary>

  No. Below the threshold frequency, no electron escapes however bright the light
  gets. Piling on more photons just means more photons that are each too weak to
  pay the work function, and their energies do not pool: an electron is freed by
  one photon or none. Cranking up red light forever still ejects nothing. That
  stubborn fact is exactly what a pure wave picture could not explain, and it is
  what forced the photon idea.
</details>
